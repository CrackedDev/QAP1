    • is a species of small, ocean-dwelling, blue sea slug of the family Glaucidae.

    These slugs are pelagic, meaning they float upside down using surface tension of the water to stay afloat, while being carried along by the winds and ocean currents.

    The Sea Swallow makes use of Countershading:the blue side faces upwards, blending in with the blue water if viewed from above, andthe silver/grey side facing downwards, blending in with the sunlight reflecting on the ocean's surface if viewed from below.

    Diet:

    Glaucus atlanticus feed on other pelagic creatures such as the Portugese Man O' War and other venomous creatures in the area. It is able to absorb stinging nematosysts to use against it's own predators.

    Fun Facts about Glaucuses!

    1. Blue glaucus can grow up to 1.2 inches (3 cm) long.
    2. Blue glaucuses can swallow air and hold it in their stomach in order to float on the water’s surface.
    3. A group of blue glaucuses floating together is called a “blue fleet.” These “blue fleets” often wash ashore and can sting people swimming in the water.
    4. Blue glaucuses lay eggs on their prey’s carcasses or other floating masses.

    Reproduction:

    All blue dragons are hermaphrodites, meaning they have both male and female reproductive organs. When two blue dragons mate, they engage carefully with long, curved, almost S-shape bends in their penises. Their long anatomy keeps them from being stung by their partner. This mating results in strings of 20 eggs that the slug lays on floating objects such as driftwood or the floating carcass of their prey.
